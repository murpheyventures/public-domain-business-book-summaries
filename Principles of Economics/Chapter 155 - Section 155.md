## Summary

Then the effect of a tax of one-tenth on Corn would be to cause its real value to rise till nine-tenths as much as before would suffice to remunerate the marginal dose, and therefore every dose. The gross Corn surplus on every piece of land would therefore remain the same as before ; but one-tenth being taken away as a tax, the remainder would be nine-tenths of the old Corn surplus. The rise in price would in fact be sure to cause an immedi- ate falling-off in the demand for some kinds of produce, if not for the staple cereals : and therefore the value of Corn, i.e. produce in general, would never full proportion to the tax, and less capital and labour would be applied 1 Compare VI. ix. These two effects would operate very much in the same way on the producer’s surplus; both would make labour dear to the employer, while the latter would also make real time wages low to the worker.
