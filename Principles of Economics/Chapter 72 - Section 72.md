## Summary

In illustrating this by a diagram, it will be well, for the sake of shortness of wording, to divide the expenses of production of a commodity into the supply prices of two things of which it is made ; let us then regard the supply price of a knife as the sum of the supply prices of its blade and handle, and neglect tho expense of putting the two together. Let ss be the supply curve for handles and SS' that for knives; so that 3/ being any point on 0x> and MqQ being drawn vertically to cut ss' in q and SS' in Q t Mq is the supply price for OM handles, qQ is the supply price for 03/ blades and MQ the supply price for OM knives. Accord- ingly we suppose that the demand for knives and the supply of blades conform to the laws indicated by their respective curves : also that the supply curve for handles still remains in force and represents the circumstances of normal supply of handles, although the supply of handles is temporarily disturbed. But in relation to the supply of the product, they co-operate with one another; being “compounded” into the total demand that carries off the supply : in just the same way as the partial demands of several classes of society for a finished commodity are aggre- gated, or compounded together into the total demand for it.
