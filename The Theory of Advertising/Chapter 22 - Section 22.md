## Summary

It is in general best to appeal to as many different classes of images as possible, for in this way variety is given and each reader is appealed to in the sort of imagery in which he thinks most readily and by means of which he is most easily influenced. But, with the growing volume of business, and with the increased need for more economical forms of transacting business, the printed page, as a form of advertisement, has superseded the market place, and is, in many cases, displacing the commercial traveler. As a first example of this, read the following advertisement (No. i), in which an entire advertisement of the Emerson piano is reproduced exactly, with the single exception that the word " incubator" is substituted for "piano." The Emerson advertisement is not peculiar because of its deficiency. The musical scale suggests music specifically; the picture of the piano recalls the sounds of the music to a certain extent; the lady at the piano suggests THE THEORY OF AD VERTISI NG music, for she is not turning around to be looked at (cf. an advertisement of Ivers & Pond pianos in the current magazines), but is intent upon her playing.
