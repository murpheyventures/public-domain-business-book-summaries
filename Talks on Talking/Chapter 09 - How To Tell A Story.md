## Summary

It requires a fine discrimination to know when to tell a story, and when not to tell one though it is urging itself to be expressed. Thus: The last time I made a speech, I went next day to the editor of our local newspaper, and said, "I thought your paper was friendly to me?" The editor said, "So it is. What's the matter?" "Well," I said, "I made a speech last night, and you didn't print a single line of it this morning." "Well," said the editor, "what further proof do you want?" Many of the best and most effective stories are serious in character. Beau Brummell was guilty in this respect, when he was once asked by a lady if he would "take a cup of tea." "Thank you," said he, "I never _take_ anything but physic." "I beg your pardon," said the hostess, "you also take liberties." There is a story that Henry Luttrell had sat long in the Irish Parliament, but no one knew his precise age.
