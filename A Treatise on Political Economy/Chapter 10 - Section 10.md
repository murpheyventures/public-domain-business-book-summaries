## Summary

For the division of labour naturally limits each operation to an extremely simple task, and one that is incessantly repeated; which is pre- cisely what machinery may most easily be made to perform. He would only divert his attention from his ordinary occu- pation, and lose time that might be far better em- ployed in his own peculiar line; besides being under the necessity of keeping up an establishment of people, horses, carriages, &c. the expenses of which would far exceed the merchant’s profit, reduced as it always must be by competition. 70 a day ; but would hardly do so, unless where there was a daily consumption of pins to that amount ; for, to arrive at this degree of division of labour, one workman must be wholly and exclusively occupied in sharpening the points, while the rest are severally engaged, each in a different part of the process. Take the village grocer ; the consumption of his groceries is so limited, as to oblige him to be at the same time haberdasher, stationer, innkeeper, and God knows what, perhaps even news-writer and publisher ; whereas in large cities, not only grocery at large, but even the sale of a single article of grocery, is a great commercial concern.
