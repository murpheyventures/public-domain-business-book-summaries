## Summary

Whenever a contract for a long prospective period was entered into, its violation could not escape detection: a person taking an obligation to pay a given quan- tity of fine silver, at a day certain, would know precisely how much silver he would have to re- ceive at the period assigned, provided his debtor continued solvent. The word gramme may be changed, but no human power can change that portion of weight actually designated by the term gramme', and, whoever shall contract to pay at a future date a quantity of silver, equal to 100 grammes weight, can never pay a less quantity of silver, without a OH PRODUCTIOH. However, it would still be necessary to enact, that no sum stipulated in grammes of silver or gold should be payable otherwise than in coin, unless under a special proviso ; else, the debtor might discharge all claims in bullion of somewhat less value than coin. With regard to obligations, they would be dischargeable in the terms of contract : an undertaking to pay 100 grammes of silver would be discharged by the transfer oi 100 grammes oi silver; unless, at the time of payment, by mutual consent of the contracting parties, any other metal, or goods at a rate agreed on, should be substituted in preference.
