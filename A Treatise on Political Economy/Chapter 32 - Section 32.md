## Summary

Foreign nations, being out’ of its jurisdiction, would of course grant no privilege to the inventor, and would, therefore, in this par- ticular, during the operation of the patent, be better ofi‘ than the nation where the invention originated. We must, therefore, elect either to go without their teas, silks, and nankeens, or be content to submit to precautions, which can alone ensure the continuance of the trade; for the dealings of individuals might en- danger the continuance of that good humor, without which the mutual intercourse of the two nations would be at an end. There is another argument of more general ap- plication, and still more frequently urged ; viz. that a company, having the exclusive trade of any given country, is exempt from the effects of com- petition, and, therefore, buys at a less price. But, in the first place, it is not true that the exclusive privilege exempts from the effect of competition ; the only competition it removes, is that of the national traders, which would be of the utmost benefit to the nation 5 but it excludes neither the competition of foreign companies, nor of foreign private traders.
