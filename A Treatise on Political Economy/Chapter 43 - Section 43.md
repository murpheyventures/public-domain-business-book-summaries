## Summary

The coinage unquestionably adds a value to the metal coined; that is to say, a lump of silver, wrought into a 5 fr. piece, is better than an equal weight of bullion of like standard ; and for a very simple reason. But the additional value, thus communicated to the precious metals by tlie coinage, must not be confounded with that, which bullion, as an article of trade, receives form the circumstance of its employment as money. On the contrary, for the purposes of importation into England, gold bullion answers every purpose of guineas ready coined, and is of just the same value, weight and standard being alike ; for the mint makes no charge for converting the bullion into coin. Its value is transmitted, for the purpose of obtaining a corresponding value (p) That is to say, to receive tlie certificate of coinage, for use, not in the character of money, but as an article of com- merce. cir. xxr.
